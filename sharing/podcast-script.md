## [0:00-0:45] INTRO

**Host A:** Today we're diving into something different — a geopolitical simulation built entirely by AI research agents.

**Host B:** That's right. We're looking at a 76-file analysis of the 2026 Iran War, modeling what happens when 18 critical resources break simultaneously. And the findings? They're going to surprise you.

**Host A:** The question isn't who wins on the battlefield. It's who remains connected to the inputs of modern civilization.

---

## [0:45-2:00] THE HORMUZ REFRAME

**Host B:** So let's start with what everyone gets wrong about this war. The Strait of Hormuz is described as an oil chokepoint. Twenty percent of global oil consumption flows through there.

**Host A:** True. But here's what headlines missed. Hormuz is actually a **polycrisis chokepoint**. Six unrelated supply chains that all feed modern civilization, all flowing through the same 21-mile-wide strait.

**Host B:** When Hormuz closed in February 2026, all six broke simultaneously. And this is where the model gets interesting.

---

## [2:00-4:00] THE SIX DISRUPTIONS

**Host A:** Let's walk through them. First: helium. Thirty-three percent of global supply went offline. Qatar's Ras Laffan complex is the world's largest helium source.

**Host B:** And here's the thing about helium — you need it for semiconductor fabrication. It's used for wafer cooling and EUV lithography. No helium also means no MRI machines, no cryogenics research, no space launches.

**Host A:** Second: fertilizer. One-third of global fertilizer trade flows through Hormuz. The Northern Hemisphere was weeks away from spring planting when the strait closed.

**Host B:** Crops went into the ground with insufficient nitrogen, phosphate, and potash. By Q3 and Q4, crop yields are already locked in. One billion people face food insecurity — and that number doesn't change even if the war ends tomorrow.

**Host A:** Third: polyethylene. Eighty-four percent of Middle Eastern plastics exports — the stuff in every food wrapper and medical package — ships through Hormuz. The food crisis has a packaging crisis stacked on top of it.

**Host B:** Fourth: sulphur. Twenty-four to forty-four percent of production was affected. Sulphur is required for copper smelting. Copper is the backbone of the green energy transition.

**Host A:** Fifth: bromine. Sixty-seven percent of global supply comes from Israel and Jordan's Dead Sea operations. Bromine is in every flame retardant, which is in every circuit board. There is no substitute that meets fire safety standards.

**Host B:** Sixth: LNG. Seventeen percent of global supply went offline for three to five years. Taiwan has eleven days of LNG reserves. Eleven days.

---

## [4:00-5:30] THE CASCADE EFFECT

**Host A:** Here's where this gets scary. Each of these disruptions is significant on its own. But they don't happen in isolation. They compound.

**Host B:** The semiconductor industry is hit by three independent input crises simultaneously. Helium at 33 percent offline. Bromine at 67 percent at risk. Neon from Ukraine war spillover. There's no single fix.

**Host A:** A chip fab can't just "find alternative helium" when bromine is also gone and neon is constrained. The model shows a 30 to 50 percent chip output cut — not from any factory being bombed, but from three separate supply chains breaking at once.

**Host B:** Same story with food. Fertilizer disruption means lower crop yields. But polyethylene disruption means food packaging fails. So food that does grow spoils faster. The model calls this a "spoilage multiplier."

---

## [5:30-7:00] INSURANCE: THE INVISIBLE WEAPON

**Host A:** Here's the most counterintuitive finding. Insurance is a bigger weapon than missiles. For every one dollar of insurance withdrawn, 390 dollars of trade freezes.

**Host B:** When Lloyd's of London withdrew war-risk coverage for Hormuz transit, traffic collapsed by 92 percent. Not because ships were being sunk. Because ships were being uninsured.

**Host A:** And here's the kicker: insurance designations persist for weeks to months after any ceasefire. The economic damage outlasts the shooting. The shooting stops. The insurance doesn't come back.

---

## [7:00-8:30] THE WINNER

**Host B:** So who wins? The US has 11 aircraft carriers. China has none.

**Host A:** But China controls rare earth processing. China refines 99 percent of the world's gallium. 83 percent of germanium. 78 percent of cobalt refining. 65 to 75 percent of lithium.

**Host B:** And China is the only major economy insulated from Hormuz disruption. When the model runs all five scenarios, China emerges as the strategic winner in four of them.

**Host A:** The US won the military war in hours. But the war was never going to be decided by who wins dogfights. It was decided by who controls the inputs of modern civilization.

---

## [8:30-10:00] PREDICTIONS AND OUTRO

**Host B:** The model produces specific predictions. Late April: the pharma cliff. Pharmaceutical buffer stocks exhaust roughly 60 days after Hormuz closure.

**Host A:** May through June: first harvest damage becomes visible in yield data. July through August: Taiwan's 11-day LNG clock becomes a live vulnerability. November 2026: three independent pressure peaks converge — US midterms, China's gallium ban resumption, EU winter gas crisis.

**Host B:** And the final finding: the $650 billion AI infrastructure buildout just got delayed by 6 to 12 months.

**Host A:** The irony: nuclear energy is the only technology both accelerated by this war and not China-dependent at the reactor level. The AI boom might accidentally accelerate nuclear renaissance.

**Host B:** All 76 files are open source. You can browse the full analysis, chat with the research, or dig into the source code. Links in the description.

**Host A:** Thanks for listening.
